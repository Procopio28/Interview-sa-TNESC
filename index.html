Guiding the Youth: A Teacher's Perspective on Drug Awareness

An in-depth look at identifying red flags, compassionate intervention, and the power of education in the fight against substance abuse.

Interview by Charles Laurence C. Cadungon • December 5, 2025

In the bustling environment of a school, teachers often serve as the first line of defense for students facing personal struggles. beyond the textbooks and lesson plans, educators like Sir Rex are tasked with a more delicate responsibility: observing the unseen battles of their students. In a recent sit-down interview, Sir Rex shared his insights on one of the most critical issues facing the youth today—substance abuse.

The conversation shed light on three pivotal areas: recognizing the early warning signs, the protocol for intervention, and the crucial role of education in prevention.

Identifying the Red Flags

When asked about the indicators of potential drug use, Sir Rex emphasized that the signs are often behavioral before they become physical. It starts with a loss of direction.

"First, they lose their proper focus. Students who are potential drug users often drift away from their academic priorities. We see a spike in absences, frequent academic lapses, and a general disregard for their schooling."

According to Sir Rex, these "academic lapses" are not merely laziness but are often symptoms of a deeper disconnect. When a student stops prioritizing their education and begins to vanish from the classroom, it serves as a critical alarm bell for educators to investigate further.

Compassionate Intervention

Identifying a problem is only the first step; addressing it requires tact and adherence to protocol. Sir Rex outlined a clear path for handling cases where drug use is suspected or confirmed. The approach is not immediate condemnation, but rather verification and assistance.

"We have to do it the right way," he explained. "First, we call their attention. We need to confirm if they are indeed using drugs."

Once confirmed, the school's role shifts from investigation to referral. Sir Rex stressed the importance of involving the "right authority"—professionals equipped to handle rehabilitation and intervention. The goal is not to expel or ostracize, but to ensure the student receives the necessary help to stop the habit before it causes irreversible damage.

The Power of Awareness

Prevention remains the most effective tool. In discussing why teenagers are vulnerable, the interview highlighted "peer influence" as a primary driver. Students often succumb to pressure because they lack a fundamental understanding of the consequences.

"We have to strengthen the education program on drug awareness," Sir Rex asserted.

He believes that ignorance of the specific "bad effects" is what leads many students down the wrong path. By clearly articulating the physiological and psychological dangers of addiction, schools can arm students with the knowledge to say no.

"If they know the bad effects," he concluded, "there is a bigger chance they will avoid it. We must let them know what they stand to lose."
